Layout Comparison

How does BitZoom's layout compare to established graph layout algorithms?
Full report with methodology, all configurations, and limitations.

Why compare?

Graph layout is a solved problem in many contexts. Force-directed algorithms like Fruchterman-Reingold have decades of refinement. Dimensionality reduction methods like UMAP and t-SNE produce excellent 2D embeddings from high-dimensional feature spaces. BitZoom takes a different approach: it positions nodes by property similarity first, uses topology as a secondary signal, and produces a hierarchical zoom structure from stored coordinates. The natural question is what you gain and what you give up.

To answer that, we ran all four methods on the same graphs and measured how well each layout preserves graph connectivity. These metrics intentionally favor topology-oriented methods. BitZoom's primary goal (property-based grouping) is not directly measured, which is an important caveat throughout.

What we measured

  • EdgeLen: How close are connected nodes in the layout? Normalized Euclidean distance between edge endpoints. Lower = tighter topology.
  • NbrP: Do a node's graph neighbors end up as its layout neighbors? Jaccard overlap at k=10. Higher = better topology fidelity.
  • Silhouette: Do known communities form visible clusters? Score vs ground-truth labels, -1 to +1. Only available for Email-EU (42 departments).

The short version

The answer depends on the graph. On dense social networks, BitZoom at high topology weight (α=1.0) produces neighborhood quality comparable to force-directed, in milliseconds instead of minutes. On sparse, chain-like graphs, five smoothing passes cannot propagate signal far enough, and force-directed produces substantially better layouts. On heterogeneous knowledge graphs, all methods struggle with these metrics because graph neighbors are often semantically different node types.

Power Grid: sparse chains

4,941 substations and generators in the western US power grid, connected by 6.6K transmission lines. Average degree 2.7, diameter ~46. The classic small-world network.

This is where BitZoom's approach breaks down. The graph is dominated by long chains, and 5 smoothing passes can propagate signal at most 5 hops. Force-directed's global repulsion/attraction model traces these chains naturally. Note that α=0.75 outperforms α=1.0: pure topology with few passes oversmooths hubs while leaving chains unresolved.

All methods score low. The reason: graph neighbors in MITRE ATT&CK are semantically different types (a technique connects to a mitigation, not to another technique). The topology metrics penalize every method here. This is the kind of dataset where BitZoom's property-first layout matters most, but these metrics do not capture that value.

What this comparison does not measure

All three metrics evaluate topology preservation. BitZoom's primary design goal is property-similarity layout: nodes that share attributes should be nearby regardless of whether they share edges. A property-similarity metric would provide a more complete picture but requires ground-truth property groupings not available for most datasets. The hierarchical zoom structure (14 levels derived from 4 stored bytes per node) is also not captured by any metric. These are real capabilities that the numbers above do not reflect.
